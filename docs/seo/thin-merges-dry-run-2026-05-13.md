<p>With so many reasons to delay the drawing of trumps, sometimes players can actually miss the most obvious line - of drawing trumps. This is not an easy hand for most people who will not be used to thinking in this way. This article is not supposed to be a speed read.&nbsp;</p>
<p><br></p>
<p>Of course at the beginning of the hand we should ask ourselves, <strong>can I simply draw trumps and make this contract? </strong>If the answer is yes, which say 50% of the time it might be, then go for it.&nbsp;</p>
<p><br></p>
<p>The thing people find more difficult, is say they realise that drawing trumps might be a bad idea at the start, they still need to ask themselves the question as the hand develops, <strong>Can I now simply draw trumps?</strong></p>
<p><br></p>
<p>The following hand is quite difficult, but uses day -to-day declarer planning skills.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p>Playing in 5♣, which happened to be the only game contract that makes, you get a diamond lead. You ruff it and decide to play two top spades, pitching a heart. Next you play the &nbsp;<strong>♠</strong> J, and west ruffs with the &nbsp;<strong>♣</strong> 9. What is your plan?</p>
<p><MakeBoard boardType="double" position="North/South" North="*S-4*H-96*D-Q1074*C-AQ8532" East="*S-*H-*D-*C-" South="*S-AKJ1086*H-K52*D-*C-J1064" West="*S-*H-*D-*C-" vuln="Nil Vul" dealer="South" bidding="_/_/_/1♠/P/1NT/P/2♣/P/4♣/P/4♠/P/5♣/P/P/P" /></p>
<p><br></p>
<p>Till this point you have taken a fairly sensible looking approach to the hand. Setting up the long spade suit, and pitching heart losers from dummy. Its now time to be very "concrete", counting winners. Lets start like this - <strong>Can I &nbsp;simply draw trumps?</strong></p>
<p>If I overruff, and <strong>draw trumps</strong> by playing the <strong>♣</strong>A. Whats going on? Worst case scenario, it only draws a single trump, leaving one trump out still (there were three out at the start of the hand) I'll make 5 clubs in dummy (why count 5 and not 6? because, we need to account for the remaining outstanding trump), 3 diamond ruffs in my hand. I'll make 2 top spade tricks and no hearts. That totals 5 + 3 + 2 = 10. I can also make 1 extra spade trick by <strong>setting up the long suit</strong>. I've already played three rounds of the suit, and have seen west show out, marking the suit as 6214. I need to play one more round of the suit to fully set it up.&nbsp;</p>
<p><br></p>
<p>So, the play goes like this. Overruff and cash the ♣A, west shows out so east has one trump left. Now ruff a diamond, ruff a spade (the suit is now set up, that was the fourth round), ruff a diamond, cash the top spade pitching a loser from dummy. Total of 11 tricks.&nbsp;</p>
<p><em>The next section was originally a separate beginner article on the same topic — combined here for easier reading.</em></p>
<p>Another example of a recent hand where asking yourself <strong>Can I draw trumps now? </strong>gives the winning line.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p>Playing in 4 <strong>♠</strong> , you get a club lead. What is the approximate plan? Assume trumps break 3-2.</p>
<p><br></p>
<p><MakeBoard boardType="double" position="North/South" North="*S-J842*H-53*D-K97642*C-7" East="*S-*H-*D-*C-" South="*S-AK93*H-A87*D-53*C-AK82" West="*S-*H-*D-*C-" vuln="Nil Vul" dealer="North" bidding="_" /></p>
<p><br></p>
<p>This is a hand where there are probably multiple ways to make the contract, but watching a world class declarer it went like this - Win it, and look to <strong>setting up your long suit</strong>. Its true dummy is weak and often your strategy will be to go after ruffs immediately, but remember you also need to use your entries productively, so playing a diamond to the King seems very productive. You play a diamond to the King which holds, and another diamond. Back comes a club, which you win, pitching a heart. Now, <strong>Can you just draw trumps?</strong> You should take the time to see that you can, using simple trick count.&nbsp;</p>
<p><br></p>
<p>Count tricks - 4 trumps in hand, 2 ruffs in dummy. 1 heart (ace), 2 diamonds (the King and the diamond you can set up), 2 clubs. That totals 11 tricks, but of course the opponents will ruff one, reducing it to 10.&nbsp;</p>
<br>
<p>So the plan goes, cash two top trumps (dummy has two trumps left). Now ruff a club (dummy will have 1 trump left), then ruff a diamond, <strong>which fully sets up the diamond suit.</strong> Now ruff a club (reducing dummy to no more trumps), and cash one of the hard earned diamonds you have set up. The opponents will ruff this diamond with their master trump, but that is 10 tricks.</p>
<p><br></p>

<p><br></p>
<p><strong>hint</strong>. Some people find it hard to count trumps tricks on hands like this. After you draw two rounds and both follow, there is one outstanding trump. You basically make 4 trumps in hand, and 2 ruffs in dummy, for a total of 6 tricks. That outstanding trump will reduce that winner count, or alternatively the opponents will ruff one of your other tricks, removing one of your winners somewhere. So you can see it as 3 tricks in hand (instead of 4), and 2 in dummy. There are more articles on counting trump tricks in the declarer play tab if this is something you want to improve on.&nbsp;</p>
<p><br></p>
